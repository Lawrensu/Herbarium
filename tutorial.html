How To Make Herbarium

Basic Step Video Recommendation

Steps

Step 1: Where To Collect

To find specimens for your herbarium, look along edges, pathways, field borders, fence lines, and railways. Avoid collecting from private property...

Step 2: What To Collect

Plant identification is best when they are flowering and press best. Ensure to collect healthy, pest-free, and dry specimens, including flowers...

Step 3: Protecting The Specimens

Protect specimens from damage during transport by placing them in sealable plastic bags and a leather satchel. Press plants immediately after collection, rehydrating stems and drying thoroughly. Store sturdier plants in the refrigerator for up to a day.

Step 4: Arranging The Specimens

Use thin plywood and parchment paper for pressing delicate wildflowers, as newsprint can pull color from blossoms. Place plants with similar thicknesses and moisture content together, minimizing overlapping parts, and isolating blooms.Use strips of parchment paper and transparent tape to hold plants in place, avoiding contact between blossoms and tape. They emphasize the importance of natural plant structure.

Step 5: Pressing The Specimens

Load plants into a press with parchment between them and plywood dividers. Apply pressure to keep plants flat while drying. Use a simple plant press with wingnuts for adjustable pressure. A stack of heavy books can work, but lacks fine-tuning for successful pressing.

Step 5: Pressing The Specimens (continued)

After 24 hours, observe plants in a press, replace parchment if wrinkling, and reassemble specimens if needed. Avoid removing paper from delicately petaled specimens. Reapply pressure and leave plants in the press for 5 days before checking again. Most plants need a week to dry, while fleshier plants may take up to two weeks.

Step 6: Mounting The Specimens

Mount plants after drying using acid-free paper and an acrylic adhesive from Lascaux. Thin with water to achieve a smooth consistency, allowing for expansion and contraction during storage. This ensures specimen preservation.

Step 6: Mounting The Specimens (continued)

To preserve the plant, avoid touching it and use tweezers and wooden skewers to hold it in place. Apply adhesive to all parts, including overlapping parts, for a full mount. Work quickly and carefully, applying adhesive to flower petals last. Place paper on overturned specimen and rub backside until stick.

Step 7: Freezing The Specimens

The specimens are mounted on parchment paper, separated by plywood dividers, wrapped in plastic foil, and strapped together with adjustable belts. The stack is frozen for 72 hours to eliminate harmful insects or fungi, while the plastic foil protects and maintains contact with the paper.

Step 8: Identifying The Specimens

Identifying plants is crucial, but often delayed due to wilting. Record observations like color, scent, and structure. Use a dichotomous key like "Flora Helvetica" in Switzerland and surrounding countries, and invest in a hand lens to magnify small identifying characteristics. Investing in a good lens can help ensure accurate identification.

Step 8: Identifying The Specimens (continued)

Identifying a plant involves comparing specimens to photographs and botanical illustrations for confirmation. Favorite sources include Hortus Eystenttensis, Gray's Lessons and Manuals of Botany, and Kew Gardens' "Botanicum" for plant anatomy and characteristics.

Step 9: Cataloging and Storage - Preserving The Specimens

Plants are assigned numbers and stored in a digital database. They are protected by sleeves and labeled with identification, botanical and common names. They are stored in wooden cases with cedar shavings for insect deterrence.
